Calanus pacificus

Calanus pacificus is a calanoid copepod that is primarily abundant in the California Current System ranging from southern British Columbia to southern Baja California. C. pacificus are omnivores that eat diatoms, dinoflagellates, and other microplankton and are eaten by larger predators like carnivorous zooplankton and fish. They are one of the most common species off the coast of California and undergo a biphasic life stage in which one part of the population reproduces all year while the other part of the population remains dormant in deeper waters during the winter.

These individuals are relatively large in their adult phase ranging from 2.5mm to 3.4mm. C. pacificus can be identified by the absence of spines on the posterior corners of the prosome as well as by the length of their first antennae which is the same length as the prosome. C. pacificus has been known to be susceptible to the White Spot Syndrome Virus (WSSV) which target shrimp and can wipe out shrimp populations rapidly. WSSV is highly contagious with the first case reported in Taiwan in 1992 and the most recent outbreak in Brazil in 2005.
