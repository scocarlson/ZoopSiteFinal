Acartia tonsa

Acartia tonsa are calanoid copepods that can be found in estuarine environments as well as in upwelling zones where nutrient abundance is high. A. tonsa can live in marine, brackish, and fresh water environments and are often introduced as invasive species in various areas through ballast water on ships. A. tonsa is found throughout the year in warmer estuarine and coastal waters. In cooler areas such as the North Atlantic, A. tonsa is the dominant copepod species in the spring and summer. Adult females also produce eggs in the winter in colder regions which eventually hatch when the water temperatures rise above 15 degrees Celsius. Like many other holoplankton, A. tonsa may avoid predators during the day by staying closer to the ocean floor and rise up to the surface at night to feed.

A. tonsa usually range from 0.5mm to 1.5mm in length and can be differentiated from similar species by their long first antennae and their branched second antennae. They also have a joint between the fifth and sixth body segments. Females are generally larger than males and have thicker abdominal appendages for egg brooding. Males have curved antennae which are used for grasping females during reproduction. Males also have five somites while females have four.
